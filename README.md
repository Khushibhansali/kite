## Concept

As an Indian living in America, I discovered that navigating two cultures often came with conflicting values, expectations, and definitions of success. Growing up, I constantly felt pulled between worlds that sometimes seemed incompatible. Over time, I realized that this tension was not something to escape, but something to learn from. Like a kite during Sankranti, I found that balancing these opposing forces is what ultimately allows you to take flight.

This project draws inspiration from **Makar Sankranti**, an Indian harvest festival celebrated in many parts of India through kite flying. Traditionally, the festival marks transition: the movement of the sun into a new celestial phase, the arrival of longer days, and the symbolic shift toward growth and renewal. During the festival, the sky fills with kites held in careful balance between wind and string. That balance became a metaphor for my own experience growing up between cultures.

For me, my Indian upbringing often represented grounding: responsibility to family, collectivism, humility, tradition, and an awareness that your choices affect the people around you. At the same time, living in America encouraged independence, self-expression, ambition, and the freedom to define success for yourself. These values sometimes clashed in deeply personal ways. I found myself translating my parents’ worldview to my friends, and my friends’ lifestyle back to my parents. I learned the guilt of saying no to family to maintain a social life, while also feeling alienated when saying no to peers in order to honor family obligations. Even success itself became a negotiation between external achievement and internal fulfillment: chasing status, money, and recognition versus creating meaningful work and giving back to communities with less privilege.

This installation explores those tensions not as universal truths about any culture, but as reflections of my own lived experience navigating identity between two environments.

The kite becomes a central metaphor throughout the piece. The wind and string are not enemies. The string does not exist to restrict the kite, and the wind does not exist to pull it apart. Both are necessary for flight. In the same way, I began to realize that my identity was not divided between two opposing worlds, but shaped through learning how to move between them.

Before the kite can fly, however, it must first be designed. The opening scene reflects how identity is often constructed through perception. Players create a kite by choosing its shape, fabric, colors, and tail. The kite may resemble an animal, an object, or a traditional diamond form. These decisions symbolize the tension between self-expression and external expectation: the version of yourself you intentionally create versus the version others project onto you.

The second scene transforms the installation into an embodied interactive experience. Using a live laptop camera feed, players physically hold and balance two virtual kite strings with their hands. As the kite rises, challenges emerge that represent moments of cultural negotiation from my own life experiences. The player must continuously balance opposing pressures to keep the kite airborne. The interaction is intentionally unstable, reflecting how exhausting and delicate identity negotiation can feel in real life.

Rather than presenting dual identity as a problem to solve, *Sankranti* frames it as an act of continuous navigation — one that can become a source of strength, resilience, and flight.

# Tech Stack

This project is built with:

* React
* Vite
* MediaPipe / computer vision hand tracking
* Webcam live feed interaction
* Interactive physics-based gameplay systems
